Kim Kulim's "Woman": A Reflection of Eastern Philosophy 
Upon arriving in Korea, I immediately noticed how individuals are constantly dynamic, whether hiking up Hallasan Mountain in Jeju with Korean grandmothers or clubbing with local college students in Itaewon. This ceaseless dynamism, so deeply in Korean society, goes beyond my personal experience and is evident in Korean local history, the Han River Economy Miracle, and rich artistic culture. Kim Kulim's works, specifically "Woman" [Figure 2], extensively explore the concept of wavering existence, an understanding of the human being as a constantly changing creature without labeling one with a mask of "identity" or "persona." Delving into the works of Kim Kulim, particularly his masterpiece "Woman" [Figure 2], provides an intriguing glimpse into the exploration of East Asian identity and how it differs from its Western counterpart. Through this essay, I aim to dissect the various dimensions of Kim Kulim's art, tracing the influence of Eastern Philosophy on the shaping of South Korean identity and drawing comparisons with a Western artist from a previous century.
Kim Kulim, born in 1936 in Sangju, stands as one of the pioneers of contemporary art in Korea (Belcher, 2022). While initially trained in the academic tradition of French expression masters, Kulim eventually broke away from the confines of art school, opting for an independent path that led him to join a vibrant creative community. At the young age of 22, he held his first solo exhibition in Daegu, marking the beginning of a journey filled with innovative artistic practices. As acclaimed critic Oh Kwang-Su, often referred to as the "Picasso of East Asia," noted, Kulim effortlessly transcended boundaries, infusing his present-day artistic practice with an experimental spirit (Carasso et al., 1991). The artist's evolution took him through the experimental art scene of Seoul during the 1960s and 1970s, a transformative stay in Japan during the mid-1970s, and eventually, several decades of artistic exploration in the United States from 1984 to 2000. These global influences, alongside his studies at the Art Students League in New York City, played an instrumental role in shaping his dynamic artistic vision and garnering international recognition. It is worth noting that "Woman" found a new home at the Korean MoMA after being acquired from a private collection in France, solidifying Kulim's status as a globally acclaimed artist (Artnet).
The painting "Woman" [Fig. 2], created in 1989, stands as a testament to Kulim's ability to navigate concrete and abstract themes. One can look for the significance of the artwork only in regards to the rising movement of East Asian avant-garde and a solid action to take up the niche. At the same time, another can see the movement encapsulated in the painting by looking at mediums. Interestingly, it is pure art (Bourdieu, 1984) without overfocusing on the context. The female body emerges from the water in a fall-like season, which depicts another level of symbolism since fall means the transition from summer to winter; the woman is not Asian. Notably, the depiction of a non-Asian woman challenges preconceived notions of ethnicity, inviting Korean art enthusiasts and Western artists to question their perceptions. The play of colors within the painting further amplifies its complexity. From soft, non-saturated shades to vibrant blues and black hands, the artwork shows various textures and contrasts, paying tribute to the movement cherished within Asian cultural philosophy. It reminds me of classic European expressionists whom Kulim was somewhat forced to be inspired by in his old school. 
Diverse patterns on the woman's body canvas and in the background symbolize constant transition, mirroring modern Korean history (Lim et al., 2013). Korea's tumultuous history, marked by foreign rulers and internal crises, spans from the Gojoseon period in the 7th century BCE to the Japanese occupation in the early 20th century, the division into North and South Korea after World War II, and the subsequent Korean War in the 1950s. Kim Kulim, inspired by the turbulence he witnessed during the Korean War in his early adolescence, sought to depict Korea's instability through intricate patterns, varied textures, and evolving plotlines in his works.
In contrast to Kulim's approach, the works of Matisse, an individual from well-established France, are characterized by plenty of bright and saturated colors. Matisse, rooted in a secure cultural and social identity, used such vibrant shades on purpose, using movement as a means to emphasize constant progression (Myers, 2010). Matisse's "Dance" [Fig.1] radiates the energy from the sharp shapes and colors. Kulim, on the other hand, embraces a different artistic perspective, weaving progression into his compositions through detailed patterns, subtle shifts in light colors, and a delicate interplay of motifs. By painting a standing white woman, he challenges traditional conventions and represents the inherent wavering existence significantly dominant in Eastern philosophy.

The principle of Yin and Yang finds eloquent expression in South Korean art. "Woman" [Fig. 2] profoundly mirrors the contrasting elements seen in not only the dynamics of the painting but also in the contrasting colors of blue and black. Typically, the combination of black and blue is an idiom for someone bruised from violence since the two colors combined form purple. It symbolizes the dualities inherent in Korean society, showing even more interconnectedness in the world of Kulim's art with history and the Korean community. Since philosophy here is shaped by Confucian values that align with Yin and Yang principles (from Chinese philosophy), the emphasis on social harmony resonates strongly in artistic portrayals. The basics are about creating a dynamic equilibrium of complex issues (Yinyang (Yin-yang)). Yin means darkness and passivity, and Yang is linked to light, activity, and assertiveness. The “Woman” (Yang) emerging from the water symbolizes contrasting elements in the painting, asserted by a nuanced color and brush shape transition. Here, Kulim navigates issues of tradition and modernity, globalization, and cultural identity. Against the backdrop of South Korea's complex political history, Yin and Yang symbolism in art serves as a poignant commentary on the division of the Korean peninsula. The contrasting forces of North and South Korea and the yearning for reunification find symbolic representation in artistic expressions influenced by Yin and Yang principles. 
In the context of Eric Hobsbawm's theories on tradition and symbolic practices (Hobsbawm, 1983), Kim Kulim's work challenges the notion of what is considered "traditional." In his work "The Invention of Tradition," Hobsbawm argues that traditions are often invented and manipulated to serve contemporary needs, including political and cultural agendas. Kulim’s fusion of modern techniques with Eastern philosophical underpinnings disrupts traditional artistic boundaries, questioning the authority assigned to specific artistic standards. Kim Kulim argues against colonization and the Western perspective so prevalent in avant-garde then. Here, as outlined by Hobsbawm, Kulim strives to create a tradition only relevant to the defined geographic area. The "wavering existence" concept in East Asia finds resonance in Kulim's portrayal of identity, where the self exists beyond definable terms. Kulim's "Woman" is a canvas that engages with Hobsbawm's ideas on class and cultural distinctiveness. By embracing avant-garde techniques and incorporating Eastern philosophical concepts, Kulim creates a work that transcends traditional class boundaries associated with art. In Hobsbawm's framework, there is a distinction between the 'primary stratum' of a tradition, which represents its official and often elite version, and the 'stratum of secondary meanings,' which allows for reinterpretation and appropriation by different cultural strata. The painting challenges notions of exclusivity and invites a broader audience to decode its meaning. It bridges different cultural strata, inviting viewers to move beyond the 'primary stratum' and explore the 'stratum of secondary meanings' embedded in the work.
Even further, Benedict Anderson's imagined communities can be see-through in Kulim's works. Anderson claims that nations are created units, and it is highly critical for members to share a strong sense of belonging (Anderson, 1991). Collective imagination is created by people like Kim Kulim, who produce specific for the geographic region cultural pieces. Since the artist's adolescence falls in times of dividing the Korean peninsula, the government is challenged to persuade the rest of the citizens of only one genuine Korea, in English referred to as "South Korea" (Kim, 2014). "Woman," through its fluidity in patterns, symbolizes how vulnerable Korean identity is and how much it lacks authority since historical categorizations removed it. Kulim's innovative approach to art, breaking away from traditional boundaries, signifies a crucial step in developing a South Korean tradition. By embracing avant-garde techniques and incorporating elements from Eastern philosophy, Kulim contributes to the region's cultural fabric, paving the way for a new identity narrative. This is a big step in inventing South Korean tradition through instruments coming from the rise of capitalism - which serves as the primary form of market in Korea since the United States achieved more meaning in local government. In essence, Kulim's contributions extend beyond the realm of art; they actively participate in constructing and reimagining a Korean imagined community.
Therefore, Kim Kulim's avant-garde painting "Woman" [Fig.2] explores the dynamic being in East Asia through the principles of Yin and Yang. The artwork rejects the labels of "identity" and navigates contrasting elements of Korean history through outstanding usage of art mediums, making it easy to identify relevant community theories. This painting contributes to the evolving narrative of South Korean identity, shaped by historical complexities and cultural distinctiveness. The painting pursues a lot of levels in harmonious existence: individual (that comes from Eastern “wavering existence”), group (which defines South Koreans existing together as a nation) and country (which determines the relationship between North and South Koreans).  It serves as a powerful commentary on the broader dynamics of globalized art. Kim Kulim was ahead of his time with “Woman” still serving as the powerful interplay of tradition and modernity.
